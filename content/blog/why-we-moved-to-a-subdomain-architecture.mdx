When we first launched Lookinit, everything lived at the same domain. The marketing site, the app, the API — all one codebase, one deployment, one domain. It seemed simple. It wasn't.

Six months in, we split the marketing site to lookinit.com and the app to app.lookinit.com. Here's why, and what we learned.

## The Problem With Everything in One Place

A combined marketing-plus-app setup creates real tensions:

**Performance** — the app needs React state, authentication, real-time updates. The marketing site needs fast static rendering and SEO. These pull in opposite directions. Trying to optimise for both in a single Next.js app means compromising on both.

**Deployment risk** — a bug in a new app feature shouldn't require redeploying the marketing site. But when they're the same codebase, they are the same deployment.

**SEO** — search engines are good at crawling static content. App routes behind authentication are noise. Mixing them dilutes crawl budget and complicates indexing.

**Team velocity** — as the codebase grew, the blast radius of any change was too large. A CSS refactor in the landing page could accidentally touch app components.

## The Architecture We Landed On

- **lookinit.com** — Next.js, statically rendered, deployed on Vercel, focused entirely on SEO and conversion
- **app.lookinit.com** — Next.js with full client-side functionality, authentication, real-time search, also on Vercel

Cross-domain authentication was the trickiest part. When a user signs up on the marketing site and gets redirected to the app, we need to pass auth state across domains. We solved this with short-lived signed tokens in the redirect URL — generated on the marketing domain, consumed immediately by the app.

## What Got Better

After the split:

- Page load times on lookinit.com dropped by ~40% — no app JavaScript, no auth checks, pure static HTML
- Lighthouse SEO score went to 100 on all marketing pages
- Deployments became safer — we can update the marketing site without touching the app
- Development is faster — contributors can work on the marketing site without needing the full app environment running

## What Got Harder

Keeping the two codebases consistent requires discipline. Shared components (like the design system) are duplicated — we haven't extracted a shared package yet, though that's on the roadmap.

Cross-domain session management adds complexity. It works, but it's more moving parts than a single-domain setup.

## Would We Do It Again?

Yes, without hesitation. The performance and SEO gains alone justified the migration. If you're running a SaaS product with both a marketing site and an app, splitting them is worth the one-time investment.
